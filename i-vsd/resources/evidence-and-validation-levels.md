# Evidence And Validation Levels

## Evidence Levels

1. Theological/Islamic validation: requires qualified scholars for finance, religious guidance, contested obligations, public harm, or high-stakes disputed cases. The skill can mark the need; it cannot supply the ruling.
2. Design validation: heuristics are applied coherently to stakeholders, principles, domains, harms, and tradeoffs. This supports design reasoning, not proof.
3. Implementation-traceability evidence: code, docs, UI, architecture, policies, or tests map a decision to principles and heuristics.
4. Stakeholder validation: interviews, surveys, usability tests, participatory design, complaints, appeals, or community feedback show how affected people experience the design.
5. Operational validation: deployed-system audits, logs, incident records, support data, security/accessibility results, moderation outcomes, and longitudinal review show real-world performance.

## Claim Wording

Use:

- “This design is traceable to the Trust and Non-Harm heuristics.”
- “The current evidence supports implementation traceability, not stakeholder or operational validation.”
- “Operational validation would require audits, incident logs, and stakeholder feedback.”
- “This issue requires qualified Islamic scholarly review before a religious-legal conclusion.”

Avoid:

- “This product is Islamically compliant.”
- “This is halal.”
- “This prevents harm.”
- “This proves users trust the platform.”
- “This architecture guarantees security, reliability, or accessibility.”

## Report Rule

Every formal output must list evidence reviewed, evidence missing, claim level, and validation gaps. If evidence is absent, say “not reviewed” rather than inferring.
